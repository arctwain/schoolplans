Reading/Writing

  • Review: A rhyme scheme is the pattern of rhymes at the end of each line of a poem or song. It is usually referred to by using letters to indicate which lines rhyme; lines designated with the same letter all rhyme with each other.
  • An example of the "abab" rhyming scheme, from "To Anthea, who may Command him Anything", written by Robert Herrick:
    Bid me to weep, and I will weep            a
While I have eyes to see.                  b
And having none, yet I will keep           a
A heart to weep for thee.                  b
  • Here is the rhyme scheme for limericks: 
    There was a young lady of Lynn,            a
Who was so excessively thin,               a
That when she assayed                      b
To drink lemonade                          b
She slipped through the straw and fell in. a
